Notes for Theodore Francis Zagaeski
Ted was the third child of Wladislaw and Klementyna. Ted was a serious youngster. He said he was constantly teased and had practical jokes played on him, with his older brother Ben often behind it.
Ted and Ben had jobs crating eggs at a local grocery, and were paid a penny a carton. Ted recalls doing all the work of assembling egg cartons while Ben loafed.
Ted attended public schools and graduated from Brighton High School in 1937.
Ted had various jobs after high school. The 1940 Census, when he was 20, lists his occupation as delivery man for a meat market.
Ted enlisted in the Army in Jan 1942, one month after Pearl Harbor. He was 22 years old. On his enlistment form, his skills were classified as vehicle maintenance and repair.
After the war, Ted attended Boston College with many others using their Gl benefits to pay for a college education. He became friends with Walter Mascioli at BC.
Ted found a job at Baird Atomic, manufacturer of test equipment for nuclear and non-nuclear applications.
Ted moved to the Army Materials Mechanics Research Center (AMMRC) in Watertown. He was co-author on a number of studies and test results related to putting various materials under extreme stress conditions.

Example: In-Plane Shear Test for Composite Materials, John M. Slepetz, Theodore F. Zagaeski, Robert F. Novello, ARMY MATERIALS AND MECHANICS RESEARCH CENTER WATERTOWN MASS. Defense Technical Information Center, 1978 - 27 pages

Ted had ambitious plans for home improvement, but was always slowed by his need to plan and prepare—that always turned into procrastination, and it infuriated Vicki. He didn’t cope well with her frequent complaints, but was surprised when Vicki decided to end the marriage.
After separating from Victoria in 1971, Ted moved to Allston MA and rented the first floor apartment at 301 North Harvard St, where his family had lived for 50 years. His sister Louise was the owner of the three-family house.
In 1983, Ted moved to Lexington MA into the home of his companion Elizabeth Murray. Betty had three children: Karen, Bruce, and Susan. Karen out of state—Florida. Bruce in Lexington, a lawyer. Susan in Arlington. In the years following Ted worked to repair and renovate the Lexington house. Betty decided to sell the property rather than maintain it. Around 1990 the couple moved to a duplex in Arlington MA which they shared with Susan.
Ted enjoyed bowling and was in leagues with his former co-workers in Watertown. He also enjoyed golf and played frequently with Betty and with their friends George and Phyllis. Ted often played at Leo Martin Course in Newton. He collected stray balls. By the time he stopped playing he had more than six hundred balls in various containers.
In 1993 Ted bought property in Vermont. Paid $75,000 for 300 acres in Thetford/Strafford VT. Almost immediately, the property was declared a hazardous waste site and EPA took control during a 10-year cleanup process. Ted talked constantly of his plan to build a dwelling on the property and harvest lumber.
In the 2000s Ted had to deal with increasing problems in Betty’s family and with his property. Betty’s daughter Susan became ill and died. Her son David became Betty’s responsibility. David had psychological problems and was a drug abuser. Betty was unable to cope with David’s problems, could not or would not tell him to leave. Over several years, David had multiple auto accidents and was arrested on DUI and possession. Betty borrowed money from Ted to cover these expenses and to pay her mortgage and utilities. The VT property remained under EPA supervision. At some point, EPA determined that soil from Ted’s property would be used to cap the clean-up. Ted was paid $175,000 as part of this settlement. During the final resolution, there was controversy over property lines being modified without notice to the town assessors as a result of EPA replacing iron pins that were removed during the clean-up. Ted had extensive documentation of his claim that acreage had been reduced and that other landowners were secretly moving these property markers and claiming EPA did it. He also believed loggers were stealing trees from his property.
In Oct 2012, after a run-in with David resulting in a broken hip, Elizabeth Murray moved to an assisted living complex. In Nov 2012, Ted vacated the Arlington house, stayed temporarily with son Peter in Iowa, then in Jan 2013 moved to an apartment in Lexington MA.
Vacating the house in Arlington was a challenging ordeal. It was necessary to remove things Ted had collected for decades. He had always acquired and saved everything. All Ted’s children gathered Thanksgiving weekend 2012 and worked for five straight days to empty the house. There was enough to fill a dumpster, with the most valuable things set aside and moved to the Lexington apartment. Later, the home owners discovered even more in the attic. Ted had stored building materials in the attic, intending to take it to Vermont to construct a cabin.
Ted lived on his own in the apartment in Lexington. Despite repeated reminders, he never contacted Elizabeth Murray again.
Ted was always precise in his life and work, at times excessively so. His attention to detail and to rules may have put him at odds with his older brother Ben and others as he grew up. He was good enough in math to be drawn to engineering. His tendency to overplan and overdesign the simplest tasks put him at odds with his wife Vicki, too. Ted was never comfortable expressing emotions or discussing feelings, and that too became a problem in his relationships at home. He had a number of male friends who appreciated his personality and humor. His cousin Stella Kenjeska described him as “the smart one” in the family. Stella remarked that Teddy was very popular with girls and had several girlfriends before and after his military service.
Ted kept much of his early life private. Among his belongings, we found many hundreds of letters he’d saved, beginning in the 1930s. Among those, we found many from a woman named Judith Reneau. I spoke to Judith in May 2014. She met Ted in 1946. She was Judith Gillman then. They met through Ted’s friend John Ahearn. She was from Chelsea, Vermont, had joined the Appalachian Mountain Club, and met Ahearn and Ted at an AMC shelter at Ponkapoag in Canton MA. Her brother was a real esate agent in VT, she had her brother help Ted find land to buy in VT. He had friends who he visited in Stow VT, that was what got him interested in land in VT, she said. She left New England in 1950 to visit her sister in Albuqueque, met her husband Reneau, and stayed. She was not in touch with Ted for about 35 years. After her husband died, she wrote to Ted at 301 N Harvard St because that was his address after the war. They were in touch off and on since the early 1980s. She visited her sister in Peabody, and Ted would see her on these trips. She said that Ted told her he met Vicki on the Cape in the early 1950s.10 11
Medical notes for Theodore Francis Zagaeski
Ted had coronary artery disease and had a quadruple bypass in 1980.
Ted was diagnosed with congestive heart failure in Dec 2013.
In December 2013 Ted was hospitalized at Lahey Clinic Burlington with difficulty breathing, and was moved to Aberjona Rehabilitation in Winchester. After returning home in late January, Ted had the help of a home health aide and personal care provided by Home Instead, Lexington franchise. He really enjoyed the companionship, despite declaring it was unnecessary.
In February 2014, Ted suffered a kind of seizure that immobilized him in bed. He was hospitalized again, recovered, and went back to Aberjona Rehab. This time his behavior in rehab was more erratic. He had difficulty with short-term memory, and had several outbursts, including confronting his roommate and demanding he get out of the room. He was evaluated as having moderate dementia. Although he recovered his general health, it was strongly recommended he needed constant supervision. His home care schedule increased to five days a week, with family taking turns supervising on weekends. He was healthy enough during this period to be taken out, and enjoyed lunching at Not Your Average Joe in Arlington.
In late March 2014, at a regular monthly checkup, his physician found a severe skin infection had affected Ted’s legs. It was decided it was serious enough to require hospitalization for antibiotic treatment. Once in the hospital, a pneumonia developed and Ted’s overall health rapidly declined despite the antibiotic treatment. Ted had a few days of clarity, and was able to enjoy a visit from his cousin Stella. His condition declined after that, and he was given palliative treatment and died at 6pm on April 6.
Military notes for Theodore Francis Zagaeski
April 2014
From the materials I’ve found in Ted’s military memorabilia, I’ve started to piece together a chronology of his wartime experiences. In 2014 I also received Ted’s Official Military Personnel File.

My starting point was finding three key pieces of information.
1) A letter written to Ted by Josephine Montenari postmarked 21 September 1942 and addressed to Battery E 209th C.A. (A.A) at an Army Post Office in NY. The abbreviation is for 209th Coast Artillery (Anti Aircraft).
2) A graduation program booklet from Officer Candidate School Class 8, Paris, for graduation on 14 May 1945, which lists Ted as having come to the OCS from the 898th AAA BN (AW) (898th Anti-Aircraft Artillery Battalion Automatic Weapons)
3) A document called a Separation Report that lists Ted as having served with the 7th Armored Division, 23rd Armored Infantry Battalion from May 1945 to Oct 1945

I have done a bunch of research and found histories of these units. It is eerie to see how closely the stories match what Dad told me about his military experience. Dad started talking about this in 1991, that’s when I made the first draft of the story. In later conversations he would re-tell the story and add in a few new bits of information. He was completely vague about details—for a guy as careful as he about recording facts, it always struck me as odd that he couldn’t tell me the units he belonged to. I’ve always been slightly suspicious about this, but I think the details weren’t as important to him as they could have been.

This is what I’ve learned.

Ted enlisted in the Army in January 1942 as a private at Fort Devens MA, a month after Pearl Harbor.

In WWII, the U.S. Army created hundreds of fighting units in different ways, including pulling state National Guard units into federal service. The 209th Coast Artillery Regiment (Anti-Aircraft) was a National Guard unit based in upstate New York. In 1941, before Pearl Harbor, the Army took it over and started adding men to it. According to the letter I mentioned, Ted was assigned to Battery E after he completed basic training in 1942. He advanced in rank to Private First Class in May 1942 just before the unit deployed overseas, then was promoted to Corporal in November 1942 in Ireland. He qualified as an Operator Fire Control and eventually as an Operator Fire Director for the unit’s anti-aircraft weapons.

The unit organized at Fort Eustis, Virginia, then trained at Camp Stewart, Georgia, and was the first anti-aircraft unit sent to Europe on 10 May 1942. They arrived in Scotland, and a week later moved to Northern Ireland. The unit spent seven months in Ireland training and defending a British naval base. On 10 Dec 1942, the unit moved to Liverpool, and about a week later departed for North Africa. It arrived at Mers el Khebir, Algeria on 3 Jan 1943. The unit’s batteries were assigned to defend locations in Algeria and Morocco during the Tunisian Campaign that ended when the Germany Army Group Afrika, Rommel’s army, surrendered in May 1943. There’s no record they saw any action during this time.

The unit continued training in Morocco during summer 1943. We have photos of Ted in North Africa, graciously shared by Ted’s cousin Katherine Kenjeska. In September, 209th was attached to the 1st Armored Division, and the whole division landed in Naples in October 1943, well after the invasion of Sicily in July 1943 and the first Allied landings in Italy in September 1943.

Ted's unit landed in Salerno. The main U.S. offensive in Italy was carried out by the huge Fifth Army moving northwest and attacking three lines of fortified positions for months, along with Allied units from France, Poland, and India. Meanwhile, the 209th was providing defensive cover for towns along main north-south supply lines north of Naples. In November 1943 the unit was defending Vitulazio and had its first major encounter with a German air raid. They shot down three Focke-Wulf 190s, the first kills of the war. In January 1944, the unit moved north to Venafro, about 10 miles east of the town of Cassino. It had its second encounter with German aircraft on 3 January 1944, and shot down four Messerschmidt Bf-109s.

From January to April 1944, the whole Allied force in Italy was stuck at the heavy fortifications along the Gustav Line, with the mountain at Cassino (Monte Cassino) at the center of this line. The 209th moved to San Pietro and to Mignano, both southeast of Cassino, and defended against German aircraft.

On 18 March 1944, commanders of the 1st Armored Division split up the 209th and created a new unit, the 898th Anti-Aircraft Artillery Battalion (Automatic Weapons). According to that second piece of information I mentioned, this was Ted's new unit. The term automatic weapons referred to the unit’s two main armaments: M1 40mm Cannon and M51 Quad Fifty (four .50 calibre machine guns mounted on a truck). They were called automatic because they were rapid-fire weapons. It took a big crew to operate these weapons.

From April to May 1944, the 898th was in defensive positions at Montesarchio northeast of Naples and then at Castel Volturno on the coast north of Naples, far away from the main battle front. In mid-May 1944, the Allies finally overcame the fortifications at Monte Cassino and at Anzio on the coast near Rome, and the Fifth Army moved rapidly north toward Rome. The 898th moved northwest along the coast, to Itri, then Borgo Faiti. After Rome fell on 5 June 1944, 898th moved north of Rome, stopping in Santa Marinella and Montaltro di Castro on the coast, then moving far north to the part of Tuscany west of Florence. It was stationed in the towns Morrona, Grosseto, Fauglia, and Valtriano.

Here’s the most interesting thing I’ve found in the online research:

"From 25 July to 13 August 1944, the 898th served on the Arno River Line as a provisional infantry battalion, engaging German infantry and mountain infantry units. Missions included defending fixed positions, ambush and reconnaissance patrols, and firing harrassing and interdictory missions with machine guns and mortars. During this period, the battalion suffered more casualties than in the rest of the war combined.”

During this three-week period Ted was wounded, along with other artillerymen who were suddenly asked to be infantry after two years of not having to pick up rifles and go on patrol.

Ted’s story was that he was on a night patrol along the Arno, searching for enemy scouts or signs of troop movement. He led his patrol through a heavily wooded area and did not see enemy troops at all during their patrol. When he returned to camp, he discovered blood running down his left leg. He had been grazed by a bullet just below the hip--he wasn't even aware he had been shot. Ted was treated in a field hospital then transported to a hospital in Rome.

He remained in Rome for three months. Near the end of his recovery, he and other soldiers in the hospital began to borrow clothes of the hospital workers and sneak out for a bit of sight-seeing. On one of these trips, Ted got together with an MP, whom he recognized from when an MP unit guarding supplies would come over to Ted's artillery unit for coffee and hot food. This MP had been stationed in Rome, and knew places they could visit for food and drink. The group hit several bars in the area. At each place, the bar owner would offer them free drinks in celebration of Rome's liberation by the Americans. Ted said he wasn't used to so much hard liquor, and after the fourth or fifth bar, he was very drunk. He said he couldn't talk, could only smile and nod his head, then stumble after the group as they headed to the next place.

Finally, they ended up at a place where the owner decided they should all come to his home for a meal. The wife served huge bowls of pasta and glasses of red wine. Ted remembers drinking half a glass of red wine and passing out face-first into the bowl of spaghetti in front of him. His buddies dragged him into a bathroom to clean him off, then dumped him on a bed, where he lay passed out for several hours.

Ted’s service record provides details. He was wounded 13 August 1944. He was transported to Rome to the 12th General Hospital battalion, and remained there until November 1944. On November 9, he left Rome. On Nov 15 he left Sardinia and arrived on Corsica, and on Nov 18 he left Corsica and arrived in France.

This matches with the story of the 898th. After the three weeks on the Arno River, in August 1944 the 898th was pulled off the front and sent south to Naples and then to Ajaccio, Corsica. The Allies had invaded northern France at Normandy in June 1944, and were pulling together another invasion force to land in southern France. From August through mid-October 1944, the 898th conducted training for new recruits joining the unit. They had to do both anti-aircraft and infantry training, because it was already clear the Allies had neutralized German air power and wouldn’t need more anti-aircraft capability.

On 24 October 1944, the 898th left the port of Propiano, Corsica aboard LST 288, and landed in Marseille two days later. The 898th moved north and on 5 November 1944 joined the 100th Infantry Division at La Salle-les-Alpes near Grenoble. The 898th would stay attached to this Division for the rest of the war. This was where Ted rejoined the 898th.

The 100th moved rapidly north close to the Belgian border, east of Paris. The 100th pushed through a defensive line near Raon-L’Etape, and during this November battle the 898th protected the division’s field artillery and engaged eight German air attacks against the infantry. On 3 December 1944 the 898th probably shot down another Messerschmidt Bf-109 over Bouxwiller in northeast France close to the German and Belgian border. During December the 898th continued to defend the 100th’s field artillery as the division tried to overcome heavy resistance near the border, but there were few air attacks. The Germans were conserving aircraft and fuel for a surprise offensive at the end of the month.

The attack came in mid-December 1944 in the Ardennes Forest northwest of where the 100th and the 898th were located, and quickly spread across a 30 mile front. This was the start of the famous Battle of the Bulge. The 100th went to defensive position, and the 898th deployed anti-aircraft defense across the entire division line. The 898th Battalion command post was in St. Louis-les Bitches on 23 Dec 1944.

At the end of December and beginning of January, in the midst of the huge battle, the 898th engaged American aircraft that were mistakenly attacking the 100th infantry positions. There is controversy about this, but most of the veterans on the ground believe the inexperienced pilots mistook the American forces for German in the bad weather conditions. The 898th had to defend the ground troops for many days of attacks by American aircraft whose pilots couldn’t determine the line between the German and American positions. The 898th also engaged real German aircraft from 1 Jan to 5 Jan and Battery B shot down one more Messerschmidt Bf-109 on 2 January. That was the last confirmed kill for the battalion. But the friendly fire attacks continued and Battery D got credit for shooting down a Mustang P-47 on 29 January.

The huge German force pushing southwest across the Belgian and German border eventually ran out of fuel and supplies, and the American forces began to surround them and push them back north. By February the Allied force was re-organizing for what they expected would be the final push into Germany to end the war. It was at this point that Ted, along with a lot of experienced soldiers, was detached from the 898th and assigned to officer training. He must have left his unit at the beginning of March 1945, because his Officer Candidate School Class 8 began near Paris on 11 March 1945, according to the graduation book I found.

While Ted was in OCS, the Allies invaded Germany and surrounded Berlin, Hitler committed suicide, and Germany surrendered and the war in Europe ended on 8 May 1945. Ted graduated from OCS on 14 May, was promoted to Second Lieutenant, and was assigned to the 23rd Armored Infantry Battalion in the 7th Armored Division in the 7th Army. Dad has a shoulder patch from this unit in his box of military things.

At the end of the fighting in May, this unit was located in the northern part of Germany near the Baltic Sea. Ted probably joined his unit at the end of May. After a month, at the end of June, the 7th moved east into Berlin, then in early July it moved to Eilenburg about 60 miles southwest of Berlin, near Leipzig. For the rest of the time it was in Germany, the unit “raided” cities and towns looking for former soldiers who had not turned in weapons, and for other suspicious individuals. It also provided security for the efforts to move civilian refugees to shelters. The unit continued to train for battle operations, since there was a possibility that forces in Europe would be sent to Asia. At the end of July the unit moved south to Buchen, southeast of Frankfurt, near the Buchenwald concentration camp. During that period, Ted visited parts of southern Germany (Bavaria), and went to Munich. He remembered the Germans being extremely friendly and hospitable, even though they had very little.

His “summary of military occupations” says:
"Infantry Unit Commander (1542). Selected and trained men for combat. Instructed in all duties leading to combat proficiency that is map reading patrolling plane spotting, physical training. Was Information and Education Officer for Battalion. Conducted educational tours to place of interest in Europe.”

In August when the war in Asia ended, the unit, along with the whole 7th Division, was sent north to prepare to leave Europe. The division left Germany in September 1945 and returned to the US, and was deactivated on 11 October 1945. That’s the date of Ted’s “separation” from the military on the separation report, too.

I’m sure the picture I have of Ted in his uniform, in front of what looks like autumn foliage, was taken when he came back to Boston in October 1945. He has infantry insignia on his jacket, not artillery, and a lieutenant bar on his cap, so this must have been taken when he returned as an officer, not before he left as an enlisted man.

After he returned to the US, Ted found work at the Howard Watch Company in Waltham. Later, he re-enlisted in the Army Reserve, was promoted to First Lieutenant, participated in mountain training in upstate New York, and used his Gl benefits to enroll at Boston College. He left the reserves in 1952.
Notes for Theodore Francis & Vittoria Trentatrieste (Family)
Ted and Vicki met some time in 1950, through mutual friends—they were never eager to talk about how they met or what their dating experience was like. [Judith Reneau, in a May 2014 phone call, said Ted told her he met Vicki on the Cape. It’s possible this was through John Ahearn or Walter Mascioli, who both had links to Cape Cod.]
They were engaged in 1951 and married in September 1952.
Their honeymoon trip was to Quebec and Niagra Falls, NY.
After their wedding, Ted and Vicki lived in Nahant, renting a floor of a house from Virginia Dougherty's father in law. After six months, they decided the mice were too bad to tolerate. They moved to a house in Swampscott, the third floor of a large house owned by a fireman. They stayed there for a year, until the neighbors complained to the town that the owner had tenants in his house. Then they moved to Vicki’s parent house on Hemenway Road for about a year, taking up a room on the second floor and sharing the rest of the house. Paul was born while they lived there.
They would have liked to stay there, Vicki's mother Gulia treated them well, but Harry was too demanding. He expected things to be done his way and complained when they weren’t. He finally asked them to move out, suggested they move to Ted's father's house in Allston.
They moved to 14 Hooker Street in Allston in 1954, and lived on the second floor. Ted's brother Ben and his first wife Helen lived on the first floor with their children. Mark was born at St. Elizabeth's Hospital in Brighton while they lived there.
ln 1956, Ted found a developer in Marblehead named Krupski, who was building new homes on the edge of town. Ted and Vicki used her savings for a down payment, and they bought a ranch-style house at 119 Lafayette Street for $20,000. They moved there in mid 1956. Peter was born in September 1956 at Mary Alley Hospital.
Some time in 1956 or 1957, Ted decided to buy a car in Detroit while on a business trip. He drove the car, a dark blue station wagon, back to Massachusetts.
Vicki became pregnant again in 1957, but the child was stillborn in 1958. The priest named him John at his baptism and last rites. He is buried at the Mattera family plot at the town cemetery in Swampscott.
Vicki became pregnant again in 1959, and Susan Marie was born at the Mary Alley Hospital in Marblehead in Feb 1960. She was two months premature and stayed in the hospital until she was almost three months old.
ln 1960, Ted traded in his car and bought a white 1960 Ford Falcon wagon.
During the 1960s, the family did many family-oriented activities. They especially enjoyed trips to Salem Willows in Salem, and to Pleasure lsland in Wakefield. Vicki took the children to King's Beach in Swampscott, where the boys were first introduced to raw mussels. They also gathered buckets of periwinkles that were taken home, steamed, and removed from their shells with sewing needles. The family would attend the beach parties thrown by Ted's company. A memorable party one year was held at Horseneck Beach in Rhode lsland, which the boys enjoyed because of the huge sand dunes and the endless supply of hot dogs, hamburgers, and soda. The family would sometimes drive to Cape Cod to spend weekends at the Mascioli cottage at Scraggy Neck, near Bourne.
About once a year, the family would go to the Museum of Science in Boston. The boys also enjoyed an occasional visit to the Peabody Museum of Natural History at Harvard. The open house at the Watertown Arsenal where Ted worked was an annual highlight, too.
Over the years, the house and yard changed. At first, Ted and Vicki made a huge garden that nearly filled the back yard, and planted flowers and bushes all around the house and fruit trees all over the yard. The garden yielded far more than the family could eat, and Vicki enlisted her mother to help preserve tomatoes and rhubarb, pickle cucumbers, and make piccalilli relish, jams, and jellies. Ted built shelves in the basement specifically for the dozens of jars Vicki filled. As the children grew and became more demanding, the garden grew smaller and was replaced by a lawn. There were always parts of the yard "under construction," and the boys spent many happy hours in the large piles of dirt. Ted engineered a famous, endless landscaping project that involved digging up a half-ton boulder in the backyard and moving it, single-handed, to the front yard to be part of a stone garden.
The basement was a great winter playroom, but gradually became filled with family possessions and the tools, materials, and other things Ted collected. The family also used the basement as a hot-weather dining room when the humidity was high. Eventually, Vicki hired a carpenter to create two rooms in the basement. She also hired a contractor to complete the circular driveway.
Ted and Vicki's relationship began to break down in the mid-1960s. They sought counselling but could not find answers to their problems. Vicki had support from friends in AA, who encouraged her to end the marriage rather than continue the conflict in the family. Vicki asked Ted to leave in Dec 1970. They remained separated for 10 years and divorced in 1981.

Their separation and divorce was painful for both, and they couldn’t communicate about family issues. They saw each other at family events, but kept their distance otherwise. However, when Vicki became ill in 1997 they had a partial reconciliation.
