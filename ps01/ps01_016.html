Notes for Enrico Agostino Mattera
Enrico (Harry) was the third of eight children of Giosue and Nicola. He was born in Calimera, a village in the south of Ischia, Italy.
Vicki Mattera: Enrico came to the US 6 Sep 1907 at age 21 on a three-year visa, not married. Worked in a restaurant at Hotel Venezia in NYC. Returned to Ischia in 1910, then emigrated to the US for good in 1914 or 1915.
Enrico married Giulia Iacono May 1913 in a double wedding in Fontana. Enrico came to the US in 1914 with his new wife Giulia and his brother Luigi. He found work as a dishwasher in a restaurant in New York.
Edward Mattera: My father Luigi was 17 when his brother Enrico was married. He desperately wanted to go with Harry and Giulia on their honeymoon trip to Pompeii. Enrico reluctantly agreed. One day during the honeymoon, Enrico became ill, too ill to leave the room where they were staying. Luigi offered to take Giulia on the planned sight-seeing trip in place of his brother. Enrico never forgave Luigi for “stealing” his bride. Enrico held a life-long grudge against Luigi. In the 1960s, when I visited Enrico in Swampscott and gave him greetings from my father, he replied, “Oh, is he still alive?…”
By 1918 Enrico was living in Swampscott at 8 Shelton Road. Enrico’s brother Michele also lived in Swampscott.14
Enrico made his living cutting grass. At first he was a laborer working for other gardeners (listed employer as Abram French in 1918)14. He was shrewd. Swampscott attracted wealthy Boston Brahmins and businessmen in the 1910s and 1920s because it was expanding as a planned community designed by Frederic Law Olmstead, designer of New York’s Central Park and Boston’s Emerald Necklace. Enrico knocked on the doors of these well-to-do homes and was hired as their gardener, cutting grass and planting bushes and flowers to improve the appearance of the homes. His business grew. During the Depression in the 1930s, Harry kept working for these well-off customers while others lost jobs and struggled. He was able to afford new cars (people in town called him Cadillac Harry). His family did not suffer the effects of the terrible economic collapse. Others in the family say Harry never missed an opportunity to remind them he was well-off while they were not.
By 1930 Harry was living at 21 Lodge Road. By 1940, Harry was able to build a new home on an acre of land at the end of Hemenway Road, land that had a greenhouse and a garage in place already (there are pictures showing them). He added another garage, planted fruit trees, and cultivated a large flower and vegetable garden. He was a shrewd investor. He was known to ask his wealthy customers for advice on investments, and began investing in stocks and bonds as the economy recovered in the 1930s, then took off during the war years and after.
Family members tell of many arguments between Harry and Giulia. Family members say they didn’t like visiting Swampscott because of the constant arguments. They say Harry was arrogant, mean, and abusive, and complained when things weren’t done the way he wanted. There are stories of times when Giulia would pack up the children and go to East Boston to escape his anger. There are stories of physical abuse against his wife and children, too.
Lydia Iacono: Harry was mean, always yelling. He terrified her. The crickets in Swampscott scared her, too. She just wanted to go back to the city. Harry and Giulia would fight all the time.
In the 1940s Harry turned over the landscaping business to his son Giosue (Joe). His moodiness became more of a burden to others. At some point in the 1950s, after the death of his son Harry Jr, he sought help from a doctor for his depression. The doctor prescribed electro-shock therapy. Harry underwent this procedure more than once. It’s not clear whether it was helpful. Some say his personality became more passive.
When Giulia passed away in early 1973, Harry was taken in by Victoria. At some point it was decided to move him to the Don Orione Home in East Boston. He died there later in 1973.
Lydia Iacono: After leaving Boston and moving to Albuquerque and California, came back to Boston, visited Harry, described the fabulous gardens in the west. Harry said, “You don't have a lot of money but you've been everywhere. I've never left Swampscott.”
John Alleruzzo: Always heard Harry was cheap SOB. John always waved to him when he’d see him drive by, but didn’t know him very well.

Notes for Enrico Agostino & Giulia Trentarosa (Family)
Enrico and Gulietta were married in 1913 in Ischia, in a double wedding with Gulietta’s sister Giuseppina (Alison Coon’s grandmother who came to US and lived in East Boston).

In 1920 the family lived at 8 Shelton Road Swampscott. There were multiple apartments in this building, according to the census, and the family had taken in two boarders who rented rooms from them.

In 1930 the family lived at 21 Lodge Rd Swampscott. There was another family in the house.18

Victoria told stories of “rag picking” at the town dump near their home. She and her sister would find clothes, shoes, and discarded household items that they would bring home.
