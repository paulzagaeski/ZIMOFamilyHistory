Notes for Klementyna Olszewska
Klementyna’s Naturalization Petition lists her birthplace as Bisowka, Russia. No modern map shows that place. It was probably in Ukraine, not Russia. She lived on a farm. Her father was widowed when Klementyna’s mother died. He soon remarried, and Ted remembers hearing that her stepmother was very good to her.

Ted recalls his mother was sent to USA to escape the poverty of the village, since there was little work and little money there. She had to borrow money from a moneylender who made a profit lending to emmigrants. It was a good business because they got good jobs in the US and sent regular payments back. She was 17 when she left home to join her sister Rose in Boston.

Ted remembers hearing Klementyna’s trip took her to the Polish railway, to the German railway, to the city of Libau, Lithuania. She traveled on the ship Birma, landed at Ellis Island in New York in 1913, and boarded the train to Boston to join her sister in the North End (Salem St). She found work at the Brigham Hospital in Boston as a cleaning woman. At some point they moved to Wellesley or Newton.

Ted remembers she decided the daily commute to Boston was too difficult or expensive, and through the Polish Social Club found a woman who needed a fourth roommate to share a Brighton apartment.

Ted recalls Klementyna met Wladyslaw at a Social Club Dance in Brighton or Newton (they could have met through a church function at St Hedwig’s Polish Church in Cambridge, where they married in 1914, just one year after she arrived in Boston). Ted heard that she remembered Wladyslaw from when he had stopped at the Olszewski farm with his father, who was a stonemason. They were travelling from town to town looking for work. Ted recalls his mother said she was ashamed of her muddy bare feet, and ran to the well for water to wash them before she would greet them. He was either 7 or 10 years older than her (his birth year was listed as 1886 but could have been 1883).

Ted recalls his mother as the sweetest, kindest, most loving person he ever knew. She never raised her voice or got angry, although she was quick to punish the boys by making them kneel in the kitchen. Ted said she often had to scold Ben for misbehaving but would always forgive him soon after. Ted said she was a wonderful cook. Ted remembers the family made many trips to Newton to visit his Aunt Rose and her family. Aunt Rose and Uncle Albin would also travel to Quincy and Dorchester to help Klementyna with her children and chores. Ted remembers she never complained about the many times they moved with the young children. He doesn’t recall that she spoke about her oldest child Stanley and his death at age 7.
Research notes for Klementyna Olszewska
Naturalization Petition cites birthplace of Bisowka, Russia
No map or gazeteer site lists Bisowka, Bisivka, Bysowka, or another version.

Modern maps show:
A small town, Byshkiv, about 50km north of L’viv (also called Byshkiv,Bishkiv,Bishkuv)
A small town, Byshev, about 90km northeast of L’viv, 250km west of Shepetivka and 300km north of Okopy

It’s possible that a roving stonemason (father of Wladyslaw Zagajewski) might travel those distances to find work. At 20km a day, it would take two weeks to travel from Okopy to Byshev and maybe three weeks to reach Byshkiv.

Ships manifest Birma 6 Feb 1913: Listed Relation: sister of R Olschewska ℅ N Shapiro, 92 Salem St Boston MA
Birthplace, Russia-Lechowjy or Lechswjy (handwritten)

Possibles:
Lechow Poland (south of Warsaw, 600km from Okopy)
Lechowka Poland (southwest of Warsaw, 475km from Okopy)
Medical notes for Klementyna Olszewska
Judy Mirabile reports Klementyna died of colon cancer at age 65 in 1961.
Notes for Wladyslaw & Klementyna (Family)
Ted Zagaeski believed there were many siblings besides his father Walter and his uncle Francis. The number was between seven and eleven. Ted's siblings believe their other uncles and aunts were lost in the Bolshevik Revolution or in World War I or ll, or sent to Siberian workcamps during the Stalin Era in the 1930s.
Ted does not know his grandfather's or grandmother's names.
