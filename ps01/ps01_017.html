Notes for Giulia Trentarosa Iacono
Family story says Giulia was born in Fontana, Ischia, although we don’t have official documentation of her birth. She was born in another Iacono family and was adopted by Antonio and Christina. She was one of five children of Antonio and Christina.
Alison Coons: “My mother's (Lydia Iacono) maternal grandparents Antonio and Christina adopted Giulia. The thing is that the villages on the island were sort of like clans. Sant' Angelo and Fontana were Iacono. Serrara was full of Matteras. Seems like there was a lot of marrying going around. Almost everyone was a Mattera or an Iacono. Gulia was adopted because her mother couldn't feed her-no milk- and gave her to an orphanage. My great grandmother Christina had lost a child, so she volunteered to feed her. The baby's parents moved to Argentina, Then, when she was weaned, the faux mother wouldn't give her back to the orphanage. Decades later her birth parents wanted her to move to Argentina and be adopted by them but she declined. Her birth name was Giulia Trentarassa (30 roses).”
Giulia married Enrico Mattera in 1913. She emmigrated to US the following year. Family story says Giulia worked at the Schraft Candy factory in Charlestown MA. It’s likely she lived in or near East Boston, where her stepsister Giuseppina lived. Her first child Nicolangela was born in 1914, and Giulia stopped working and remained a housewife from then on.
By 1918 she was living in Swampscott MA at 8 Shelton Road.￼ Two more children, Giosue (1917) and Vittoria (1918), then a long gap until the birth of Enrico Jr in 1929.
From family stories, it seems Giulia had typical responsibility for the home and for the children, and also had to cope with her husband’s quick temper and demanding personality. When the family moved to the “big house” on Hemenway Road in 1940, Giulia managed the garden and the animals, chickens and rabbits raised for food.
Family stories avoided talking about the difficulties Giulia faced during the 1940s and 1950s. Besides coping with a difficult husband, Giulia’s fourth child, Enrico (Harry), had significant emotional problems as he grew up in a tension-filled household. It’s not known how Giulia felt about his suicide in 1954—the issue was too shameful to be talked about. Paul Zagaeski remembers hearing her say, “Harry good boy. He buy you ice cream cone,” whenever he asked about Harry.
Paul recalls, as a pre-schooler in the mid-1950s, spending the day with Giulia a few times while Victoria did errands. Giulia was nearly silent during these visits, hardly ever smiled, and her most frequent words were “aspet’” and “mang’” (Neapolitan for “aspettare”, wait, and “mangiare”, eat). Paul recalls being amazed by how she slaughtered a chicken in the basement, over a large stone sink. Paul recalls her voice being extremely hoarse, as if she shouted every day. When she spoke to Victoria or another family member, the hoarseness made it seem like she was shouting. The only time she shouted, though, was when she argued with her husband Harry. These arguments would go on and on and were repeated constantly.
Paul recalls watching day-long canning sessions in Giulia’s kitchen, when she would preserve tomatoes and fruits. The basement had large shelves with dozens of Ball jars filled with preserved food. Paul also remembers walking with Giulia to the nearby Fisherman’s Beach, where she would stand and watch the local fishermen unload the day’s catch from their small boats. She would pounce whenever she saw something that would not be sold to a local fish market or restaurant, like halibut, octopus, or squid. She would haggle like crazy and usually get it for less than a dollar. Paul recalls the first time he saw Giulia prepare an octopus in a tomato sauce and serve it over pasta.
Because she spoke almost no English, Giulia did not have any American friends. She visited relatives in East Boston or other towns only when Victoria was able to bring her there. She didn’t smile often—she’d lost many teeth and must have been self-conscious of her appearance. She gave the impression of being resigned to live out her days doing household chores and taking care of an unpleasant husband.
As she approached her 80th year, her health began to fail. In April 1973 she fell unconscious, spent a few days in the hospital, and suffered heart failure. She was laid to rest in the family plot at Swampscott town cemetery with her son Harry Jr.
Vicki said her mother was always kind and warm-hearted. Giulia admired the Indian statesman Ghandi because he was a champion of the poor and oppressed. The photos we have show her unsmiling and rather grim—she only looks happy in one picture with her stepsister Giuseppina in East Boston.
Notes for Enrico Agostino & Giulia Trentarosa (Family)
Enrico and Gulietta were married in 1913 in Ischia, in a double wedding with Gulietta’s sister Giuseppina (Alison Coon’s grandmother who came to US and lived in East Boston).

In 1920 the family lived at 8 Shelton Road Swampscott. There were multiple apartments in this building, according to the census, and the family had taken in two boarders who rented rooms from them.

In 1930 the family lived at 21 Lodge Rd Swampscott. There was another family in the house.18

Victoria told stories of “rag picking” at the town dump near their home. She and her sister would find clothes, shoes, and discarded household items that they would bring home.
