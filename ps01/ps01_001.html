Notes for Wladyslaw Zagajewski
Family name was ZAGAJEWSKI. Ted remembers that an employer wrote the name wrong, and Walter didn't bother to fix it. He also used Walter as first name. Family and friends called him Waldi. Research indicates more than 600 individuals named Zagajewski immigrated to the US from 1870 to 1920.
On his naturlization papers, Walter identified “Okop” as his birthplace. Wikipedia identifies a village in western Ukraine named Okopy (Polish: Okopy Świętej Trójcy, translation “Ramparts of the Holy Trinity”). The village was on high rocky ground at the joining of the Zbruch and Dnister Rivers. It surrounded a fortress built in the late 1690s by a Polish prince against attacks by the Turks. The village and the surrounding region, called Galicia, shifted from Poland to Austria in late 1700s. After much turmoil in the late 1800s and early 1900s, the village came back to Poland in 1920. Between the World Wars, the area thrived with exports of fruit and wine. But, according to Wikipedia:
“…After the World War II, the site was annexed by the Soviet Union. The village was renamed "Okopy" and was turned into a Kolkhoz (collective farm), and soon totally depopulated, as a result of the forced migration of Poles to Siberia….”
Google Maps link for the village of Okopy, Ukraine.
Photos of the village of Okopy, Ukraine.
Ted remembers hearing Walter mention towns of Slavuta and Shepetivka when talking about his childhood (Slavuta is 250km from Okopy—150 miles). He remembers hearing that Walter had many siblings, that there were anywhere from seven to 11 children in his family.
Ted remembers hearing that Walter was forced into the Russian army around 1908 when he was 21. When the Russians began to prepare for the conflict with Austria that became World War l, he was certain he would be drafted again. He emigrated to the US in 1911 at age 24.
Ted remembers Walter arrived at Ellis lsland New York and traveled to Baltimore when he first arrived, but does not know who his sponsor in Baltimore was (see research note). Walter got a job with the Gas & Electric Company as a pipe layer. Not liking that work, he moved to Boston and found work in a textile mill in Newton Lower Falls. After getting married in 1914, he got a job at a leather mill in Georgetown MA. He had heard there was good money in leather work. He moved his family to Georgetown for a year. The job was to shred leather scraps and press them into sheets. He came home covered in dye that took an hour of scrubbing to remove. After a year, he got a job as a painter at the Boston & Albany railyard in Allston. He liked that work because it was indoors and not as dirty as mill work.
Ted thinks Walter had a trusted friend or relative in the area. Ted recalls that around 1920 he came to Walter with a "sharp deal" to buy a house cheaply. He had found a house in Quincy that was to be demolished. The person bought the house and sold it to Walter so it could be moved instead of demolished. Walter moved his family there, and they lived in it while a foundation was laid at a vacant lot nearby. During those months he and his wife Katherine, taking care of Stanley and Ben and pregnant with Ted, lived without water or electricity. Blanche LeBrun recalls visiting that house. She recalls taking the train from Newton to Quincy with her mother Rose to help her aunt Katherine with the babies at “the house with no water or electricity.” The house was moved to the new foundation over a three day period, and the family continued to live in it during the move. When it was finally placed, Walter hooked up the power and water connections himself. The building was large enough to rent out the lower floor as a store. Ted recalls there being a garden in the back of the house. The family lived there for three years, with Walter taking the streetcar from Quincy to Park Street Station, then to Allston and back every day. No one knows the address of this building.
In 1923 Walter bought a house in Dorchester on Dorchester Ave. Ted recalls they had to leave the house in Quincy before the owners of the house in Dorchester moved out, so the family had to live in an unfurnished apartment for six weeks. Ted remembers having to sleep on wooden pallets and being very unhappy during that time. He remembers going to kindergarten in Dorchester that year. The house was a six-family duplex with tenants living in the other five apartments. It became too difficult to maintain.
In the late 1920s, Walter bought the houses at 299 and 301 North Harvard Street to be closer to work in Allston. He also bought the lot next door to prevent another house from being built there. He and a man named Bubinski were partners in this deal because Walter couldn't finance it alone. Kay Bubinski Hannon still lived at 299 North Harvard as of 1991. Walter bought the empty lot from an Italian who had hired an Irish contractor to dig a foundation. Ted remembers spending a summer or two shoveling the dirt back into the hole. Ted remembers how they made a large garden in the open lot. For fertilizer, Walter would buy loads of manure from the stockyards in Brighton, and his sons would have to go to the yards with a wagon, load it with manure, and push the wagon back to their house. Ted recalls finding this embarassing.
Ted recalls that during Prohibition in the 20s, Walter made white lightning whiskey in the basement and sold it to friends and neighbors.
Walter had lied about his age, so he worked until he was actually 70. The rail yards in Allston were closed when he was around 62, and he was given a job in the Albany yards. He had to travel to Albany on Sunday evenings. He rented a room at the home of a Polish couple there, and stayed there Monday through Thursday nights. On Friday he would travel home. Ted remembers picking him up at the train station. Louise and Terry would usually pick him up, or Blanche and Sonny sometimes.
Ted remembers that although Walter only finished the third grade, he was clever and taught himself many skills. He also recalls Walter's quick temper and harsh punishments.

Military notes for Wladyslaw Zagajewski
According to Ted, Walter was drafted into the Russian Army and was sent to fight in the Russo-Japanese War of 1904-1905. He recalls hearing Walter describe riding the Trans-Siberian Railroad to Vladivostock on the Sea of Japan. Ted recalls hearing about a return trip that involved pulling barges on a river or canal. Ted says Walter emmigrated to the US to avoid being redrafted as Russia prepared for war with Austria.
Notes for Wladyslaw & Klementyna (Family)
Ted Zagaeski believed there were many siblings besides his father Walter and his uncle Francis. The number was between seven and eleven. Ted's siblings believe their other uncles and aunts were lost in the Bolshevik Revolution or in World War I or ll, or sent to Siberian workcamps during the Stalin Era in the 1930s.
Ted does not know his grandfather's or grandmother's names.
