Notes for Vittoria Trentatrieste Mattera
Victoria was the third child of Harry and Giulia Mattera. When she was born, the family lived at 8 Shelton Road Swampscott MA. Growing up, she was close to her older sister Nicoletta and the two found many playmates among the immigrant families that settled in the town. Among her strongest childhood memories were frequent trips to the town dump not far from their apartment, where children would go “rag picking” to find cast-off clothes and toys from the town’s well-to-do families, as well as gathering unburned coal from ash piles to fuel the family’s coal-burning stove.12 In summer, she would spend long days at Fisherman’s Beach near her home.

Vicki attended public school in Swampscott and graduated from Swampscott High School in 1936. She followed her sister’s example by applying for secretarial jobs, where she used office skills she had learned in high school that were in high demand at the time—typing, stenography, bookkeeping, and telephone manners. By 1938 she was working at Sylvania Lighting, which had production plants in Lynn and Salem. She was secretary to the salesmen who sold lighting used in the hundreds of factories and other businesses that were part of the war-time and post-war economic boom.

Vicki and her sister had a large group of friends and were always taking part in the social activities for young men and women that were popular in the 1930s and 1940s. They went to church socials, picnics, concerts, beach outings, and other public events. They would often go to the dances held at the oceanfront hotels. The most famous was the New Ocean House Hotel, where hundreds of young people would flock to hear the stars of the time, including Benny Goodman and other big bands. Lynn was where the General Electric Company was founded and had large war-time production facilities, so thousands of workers came to the area, and the city became a hotspot of restaurants and nightclubs. Vicki and her sister made new friends in Lynn, including the Tagliamonte family, Jean Psehoyas, and the derBedrosian sisters. Vicki would talk about catching the streetcar in Swampscott that would take her to downtown Lynn.



Vicki was particularly close to the Buffalino family of Swampscott. Her best friends were Virginia and Eleanor Buffalino. The Buffalino sons, Raymond and Harry, were “college boys” in the 1930s, and Vicki talked about the trips she and Virginia would take to the Ivy League towns, including New Haven and Ithaca NY, to join the Buffalino boys for Ivy League football weekends. She talked about taking a train to Ithaca for a weekend at Cornell University as one of the highlights of that time.

Unfortunately, this kind of behavior was never accepted by Vicki’s father Harry. He was frustrated at the behavior of his two daughters who did not stay at home, get married, and tend to a house. Vicki and her sister Nicoletta were constantly shamed and berated. Vicki talked about how they always had to sneak back into the house after being out in the evening, and how they suffered verbal and even physical abuse when he confronted them. She said her mother would intervene and Harry would turn on her and give her the same treatment. Vicki didn’t like to talk about it, but she described the constant tension in the house.

At the same time, Vicki and her family were fortunate that Harry’s gardening business remained successful through the Depression years. Since Vicki had her own income, she was able to dress well and be active socially.

Vicki experienced the war years the way many at home did—she invested in war bonds, collected for scrap drives, and waited for news about the men from Swampscott serving in Europe and the Pacific. When the war ended and the men returned, she hoped to find someone eager to marry her.

Vicki dated until she was in her early 30s. She met Ted Zagaeski on Cape Cod in 1951 when she was 32. Vicki liked his family, especially his mother and his sister Louise. They were married in September 1952. Vicki stopped working and had her first child in 1953.

Vicki never talked about what her family endured when her youngest brother Harry Jr killed himself in the family home in 1954. It’s hard to imagine now, but at the time such an event was never spoken of again in a family. It must have had a devastating effect on her.

After a few years moving between apartments, in 1956 Vicki moved with her husband to a newly built house on Lafayette St in Marblehead. She soon had three children to care for, and threw herself into being a 1950s mother and wife. She learned to cook from reading Betty Crocker recipes. She always had an evening meal ready to serve as soon as Ted came home. She was a perfectionist about housekeeping and spent hours cleaning the house and washing clothes. She hung laundry on an umbrella-style four-sided clothesline in the back yard. She adopted many frugal practices of Italian wives, including buying food in bulk or growing it in a garden and then preserving it. She most often preserved peaches, rhubarb, and garden tomatoes to use in the winter and spring.

Vicki had started using tobacco in the 1930s, and remained addicted to cigarettes all her life. It was not uncommon in the 1950s and 1960s for mothers to smoke in the home around children. For Vicki, this was normal. It was unusual to see her without a cigarette.

Vicki was a devoted church-goer, and made sure the family attended Sunday Mass at Star of the Sea church in Marblehead. She enrolled her sons at the church school, also. It was far from convenient, but she felt it was important to give the children religious education.

Vicki was a dutiful daughter. Her older sister Nicoletta had rejected the family and moved away, so Vicki must have felt a strong obligation to help her parents. She took on day-long window cleaning chores at the Hemenway Rd house every six months. She spent hours with her mother preserving fruit and tomatoes and making sausages and tomato sauce. She also endured constant arguments between her parents, and would be dragged in to defend her mother and try to settle their disagreement. This was hard on Vicki—she didn’t want to be in the middle, but couldn’t help herself.
Vicki also took her mother to visit Italian friends and relatives. Her mother’s sister Giuseppina lived in the North End of Boston, and her mother’s brother Stanislao Antonio (Nino or Ni) lived in East Boston. There were others from her parents’ villages in Italy who lived in Lynn, Salem, Saugus, and Rhode Island, and Vicki would take her mother to visit them. One was Romilda of Salem—we don’t know her last name.

One time, a very old woman (her name unknown) walked from her home in Lynn to Vicki’s house in Marblehead just to deliver garden vegetables. Vicki was astonished and tried to make the woman wait until Ted came home in the evening so she could drive her back to Lynn, but the woman refused and walked all the way back.

Vicki began to experience difficulties in the late 1950s. She lost a child in 1958—it was a stillbirth, and in hindsight it may been related to her tobacco use. She immediately became pregnant again, and this time the child, Susan, was nearly two months premature and was not expected to survive. The following year was very difficult, as she cared for the sickly baby while keeping three active boys in line, being an obsessive housewife, and coping with her parents. Her sister Nicoletta, who had been devastated both by her brother’s violent death and a failed relationship, turned against her father Harry and tried to get Vicki to condemn him as well. In 1962 Nicoletta left Massachusetts permanently, but continued to pressure Vicki, who felt trapped between her parents and her sister’s anger at them.

Meanwhile, ambitious plans to improve the Marblehead house were becoming another problem, since Ted insisted on doing all the work by himself, which meant long delays and false starts. One related incident stands out—the washing machine failed, and Ted insisted he could buy the parts and repair it. It took months for him to do it, and Vicki experienced daily frustration going to a laundromat while her washing machine collected dust in the basement.
Vicki began to use alcohol to cope with the pressure she felt. Gradually, glasses of wine turned to bottles of vodka. Fortunately, a friend, Jean Chaplik, persuaded Vicki to attend an Alcoholics Anonymous meeting. Vicki adopted AA as her path to sobriety and also as a source of counseling on what to do about the problems in marriage and family.
After five more years trying to reconcile with Ted, in 1970 Vicki asked him to leave. In the following years, Vicki renewed herself. She took a job preparing and serving lunch in the Marblehead High School cafeteria, and later packed candy boxes at Harbor Sweets in Salem MA. She also took on secretarial work.

Her secretarial work was transcribing notes for Jean Chaplik’s husband, an orthopedic surgeon. She began typing the notes from Dictaphone recordings using her Royal 10 typewriter from her earlier secretarial work. In 1982 Dr Chaplik bought a $10,000 IBM DisplayWriter word processing system and insisted she learn to use it. She struggled for weeks but eventually learned it well enough to produce the transcriptions on a dot matrix printer.
Vicki was able to take advantage of an inheritance to complete repairs to her house and improve it. She was most proud of the enclosed sun porch, from which she could watch birds in her garden, and of the coal stove she put in her living room, which let her keep the house cozy in the winter. She knitted and crocheted constantly, and everyone she knew received knitted clothing as gifts.

Vicki was devoted to gardening, and kept a large garden in Marblehead. She was especially fond of homegrown tomatoes, basil, and Italian beans, and would try new vegetables and flowers every year to find new favorites. She would spend weeks planning in the winter months, and once the snow disappeared would spend as many hours as possible planting, cultivating, and watering. She used to say her favorite way to start the day in summer was to take her coffee out at daybreak, barefoot, dig her toes in the dirt, and see what had blossomed that morning.
In the 1970s, she decided to re-establish connection to her uncle’s family in New York. In 1976 she invited as many Matteras as she could to a Bicentennial Independence Day cookout in Marblehead. It was the first time some of her cousins had seen her and her kids. At this even she also invited her Mattera cousins Marie, Laura, and Palmer, who lived nearby in Swampscott, for the first time to visit her home. It was a very successful get-together that helped create a link to her New York cousins that lasted the rest of her life.

Beginning in the 1980s, Vicki travelled to visit family, and to vacation in warm climates. She went on cruises to Bermuda and Florida. She travelled to New York to attend opera with her cousins Joe and Eddie. She travelled to Italy with her brother Joe and visited her father’s family in Ischia. She travelled to Oklahoma to visit her cousin Frances. Her last cruise was to Alaska to spend a week with a local radio personality whose gardening show she listened to every week.

In the early 1990s, as she entered her 70s, Vicki began to feel the house was becoming too much for her to handle. She was committed daily to care for Susan’s son Keegan, which left her with little time for the kind of housekeeping and maintenance she was used to doing. Peter stepped in with a plan to move in with his wife and son, expand the house dramatically, and create a separate living space for Vicki. The intention was good, the plan was good, and the execution went well. But it cost more and took longer than expected. As time went on, Vicki clashed often with Peter and his wife, who valued privacy and couldn’t adjust to Vicki’s constant involvement in their homelife. It became necessary to sell the house, and Vicki moved to an apartment on Vine St in downtown Marblehead.

Vicki continued to care for Keegan and work part-time. She kept in touch with friends, and visited with her brother Joe often. She began to experience leg pain and shortness of breath. She complained of these symptoms but avoided medical attention. When she finally relented and visited her doctor, tests showed she suffered from emphysema. She had medication and oxygen, which helped her stay active. She continued to live on her own and care for Keegan.
After more than a year of avoiding follow-up, tests showed Vicki had developed lung cancer. She went on chemotherapy, and made the difficult decision to leave her apartment and move to Susan’s house. The chemotherapy was effective and Vicki had several good months. She spent a month with Peter in Sterling, to give Susan a break from her daily care. Shortly after she returned to Susan’s house, in November 1997, she passed away in her sleep.

Vicki had great capacity for empathy, for caring about someone else’s needs, which she inherited from her mother. She was very funny, often unintentionally, and she could get very enthusiastic about something she wanted to share. She could be shy when in unfamiliar company, but more often she found herself reaching out to someone she’d just met, telling them personal stories and asking them to share their own. She could disarm and engage just about anyone. She could be too intense when it came to personal or family matters, and she would take it personally when someone disagreed with her about something she was sure about. Like her mother and her sister, she wanted to have emotional allies in a family dispute to overwhelm the one they opposed—probably because she’d learned it wasn’t enough to simply stand on your own against an unreasonable person like her father. She would always negotiate, but she found it very hard to compromise. She was very affectionate, and like many, she often showed it indirectly, with gifts and favors. She really enjoyed entertaining and being entertained—she was the life of the party when she was with people she liked. She enjoyed her garden, her pets, any kind of opera, and getting someone to try food she’d made and to say it was delicious.

Medical notes for Vittoria Trentatrieste Mattera
Vicki was often troubled by depression. For about five years in the early 1960s she was also troubled by alcohol abuse. Fortunately she found AA and was able to cope with her alcohol addiction from 1965 on. VIcki was diagnosed with breast cancer in 1970 and underwent radical surgery to treat it. Vicki was a heavy smoker beginning in her teen years. She was fortunate to avoid health consequences for many years, but was diagnosed with emphysema and lung cancer in her mid-70s.
Notes for Theodore Francis & Vittoria Trentatrieste (Family)
Ted and Vicki met some time in 1950, through mutual friends—they were never eager to talk about how they met or what their dating experience was like. [Judith Reneau, in a May 2014 phone call, said Ted told her he met Vicki on the Cape. It’s possible this was through John Ahearn or Walter Mascioli, who both had links to Cape Cod.]
They were engaged in 1951 and married in September 1952.
Their honeymoon trip was to Quebec and Niagra Falls, NY.
After their wedding, Ted and Vicki lived in Nahant, renting a floor of a house from Virginia Dougherty's father in law. After six months, they decided the mice were too bad to tolerate. They moved to a house in Swampscott, the third floor of a large house owned by a fireman. They stayed there for a year, until the neighbors complained to the town that the owner had tenants in his house. Then they moved to Vicki’s parent house on Hemenway Road for about a year, taking up a room on the second floor and sharing the rest of the house. Paul was born while they lived there.
They would have liked to stay there, Vicki's mother Gulia treated them well, but Harry was too demanding. He expected things to be done his way and complained when they weren’t. He finally asked them to move out, suggested they move to Ted's father's house in Allston.
They moved to 14 Hooker Street in Allston in 1954, and lived on the second floor. Ted's brother Ben and his first wife Helen lived on the first floor with their children. Mark was born at St. Elizabeth's Hospital in Brighton while they lived there.
ln 1956, Ted found a developer in Marblehead named Krupski, who was building new homes on the edge of town. Ted and Vicki used her savings for a down payment, and they bought a ranch-style house at 119 Lafayette Street for $20,000. They moved there in mid 1956. Peter was born in September 1956 at Mary Alley Hospital.
Some time in 1956 or 1957, Ted decided to buy a car in Detroit while on a business trip. He drove the car, a dark blue station wagon, back to Massachusetts.
Vicki became pregnant again in 1957, but the child was stillborn in 1958. The priest named him John at his baptism and last rites. He is buried at the Mattera family plot at the town cemetery in Swampscott.
Vicki became pregnant again in 1959, and Susan Marie was born at the Mary Alley Hospital in Marblehead in Feb 1960. She was two months premature and stayed in the hospital until she was almost three months old.
ln 1960, Ted traded in his car and bought a white 1960 Ford Falcon wagon.
During the 1960s, the family did many family-oriented activities. They especially enjoyed trips to Salem Willows in Salem, and to Pleasure lsland in Wakefield. Vicki took the children to King's Beach in Swampscott, where the boys were first introduced to raw mussels. They also gathered buckets of periwinkles that were taken home, steamed, and removed from their shells with sewing needles. The family would attend the beach parties thrown by Ted's company. A memorable party one year was held at Horseneck Beach in Rhode lsland, which the boys enjoyed because of the huge sand dunes and the endless supply of hot dogs, hamburgers, and soda. The family would sometimes drive to Cape Cod to spend weekends at the Mascioli cottage at Scraggy Neck, near Bourne.
About once a year, the family would go to the Museum of Science in Boston. The boys also enjoyed an occasional visit to the Peabody Museum of Natural History at Harvard. The open house at the Watertown Arsenal where Ted worked was an annual highlight, too.
Over the years, the house and yard changed. At first, Ted and Vicki made a huge garden that nearly filled the back yard, and planted flowers and bushes all around the house and fruit trees all over the yard. The garden yielded far more than the family could eat, and Vicki enlisted her mother to help preserve tomatoes and rhubarb, pickle cucumbers, and make piccalilli relish, jams, and jellies. Ted built shelves in the basement specifically for the dozens of jars Vicki filled. As the children grew and became more demanding, the garden grew smaller and was replaced by a lawn. There were always parts of the yard "under construction," and the boys spent many happy hours in the large piles of dirt. Ted engineered a famous, endless landscaping project that involved digging up a half-ton boulder in the backyard and moving it, single-handed, to the front yard to be part of a stone garden.
The basement was a great winter playroom, but gradually became filled with family possessions and the tools, materials, and other things Ted collected. The family also used the basement as a hot-weather dining room when the humidity was high. Eventually, Vicki hired a carpenter to create two rooms in the basement. She also hired a contractor to complete the circular driveway.
Ted and Vicki's relationship began to break down in the mid-1960s. They sought counselling but could not find answers to their problems. Vicki had support from friends in AA, who encouraged her to end the marriage rather than continue the conflict in the family. Vicki asked Ted to leave in Dec 1970. They remained separated for 10 years and divorced in 1981.

Their separation and divorce was painful for both, and they couldn’t communicate about family issues. They saw each other at family events, but kept their distance otherwise. However, when Vicki became ill in 1997 they had a partial reconciliation.
