Notes for Ann Cupolo
Ann Cupolo Freeman There was a down side to moving to Berkeley. I missed many years with my parents, and I didn't see my younger siblings and my nieces and nephews grow up, or spend time with my cousins, uncles and aunts. A once a year or so visit didn't cut it. Berkeley was like a trap - Once I moved here and experienced physical independence, a wonderful disabled community, an "independent living" mindset, higher expectations for my life, and the better weather, I couldn't go back to being dependent. I'm glad that life has improved for people with disabilities in NY over the years.

2015-01-02 9:29 PM—As you know, I'm the oldest - I'm 62. Grace is 61 and has two kids, Kate and Sean. You know Joan's kids. Joan is 57. Mike is going to be 53 this month and he has one son. Joseph would be 52 this May and has a son and a daughter.
I am retired now - I was a licensed clinical social worker (license is kept on inactive status in case I want to get another social work job someday). Went to UC Berkeley for my master's in social welfare in 1982. I worked primarily as a hospital social worker in physical medicine with people who had strokes, brain injuries, spinal cord injuries, etc. who were going through physical rehab. I also worked in an independent living center for people with disabilities. Currently, I'm on the board of directors of the Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund, Inc. in Berkeley and I volunteer in a 1st grade class in a Berkeley public school helping kids with reading and writing. I love it. I stopped working about 6 years ago because of increased joint and neurological pain that comes about as people with my disability age. It's all managed fairly well, and I'm really doing OK. Sam is 65 and recently retired but works occasionally as a consultant. He's an electrical/hardware engineer. We met at a Little People of America National Convention in San Francisco in 1992.
I actually followed friends from NY and from Hofstra University in NY to Berkeley. In 1975, it was one of the most wheelchair accessible cities in the country and this was before there were disability rights laws that access. It was Berkeley-type activism that made things happen here. I could get around in my motorized wheelchair so easily here! And then of course, it doesn't snow in the Bay Area except occasionally on the hills. Makes it much easier to get around here than in NY.
